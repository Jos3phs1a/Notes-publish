# Overview

because of attachments of various muscles at different angles along femur and hip, many muscles can have secondary contribution to movements in different planes

e.g. adductor brevis primary → adduction secondary → hip flexion
adductor magnus primary → adduction, secondary → hip extension etc

factors that affect secondary roles include:
- number of joints crossed
- hip position
- fiber orientation
- moment arm
- [[Hip movement#femoral-on-pelvic hip movement|femoral-on-pelvic]] or [[Hip movement#pelvic-on-femoral hip movement|pelvic-on-femoral]] movement
- external load


# Flexors

## Force couple
Between **Hip flexors** and **trunk extensors** 
causing anterior tilt of pelvis + extension/lordosis of lumbar spine

**Iliopsoas muscle group** is one of the strongest hip flexors (along with rectus femoris)
functionally:
- prevents us from falling backwards
- is the ==only hip flexor== producing enough force for hip flexion in >90° in sitting
- [[Concentric stabilizers]]

## Application
1) **Tight Hip flexors**
 - result in low back stress/injury:
	- if abdominals not strong enough, cannot stabilize trunk during [[Hip movement#femoral-on-pelvic hip movement|femoral-on-pelvic movement]] e.g. straight leg raise → lumbar lordosis 
- flexor contractures ^febbf8
	- higher demand on hip extensors to stabilize trunk
	- optimal overlap of regions with thickest articular cartilage cannot occur, causing wear and tear → osteoarthritis
	
2) **Weak hip flexors
	- tendency to fall backwards
	



# Extensors

## Force Couple
Between **Hip extensors** & **Abdominal muscles** 
causing posterior tilt of pelvis + flexion/loss of lordosis of lumbar spine
limited by [[Hip joint stability#^1365a7|iliofemoral ligament]] 

## Application
1) hip hinge movements e.g. deadlift, bending forward → ↑ distance between hamstring + hip → ↑ moment arm → ↑ torque/loading 
	note: deadlifts/RDLs mostly targets hamstrings to begin with, glute "bias" aka changing your forward lean angle does v little (just change exercise)
2) Loaded hip extension requires secondary muscles e.g. back extensors + adductors to activate

### Torque generation 
**gluteus medius** is strongest hip abductor in body, due to distal attachment to greater trochanter, projects line of pull laterally by ==5-7.5cm==, increasing moment arm. 
Greatest torque is also at lengthened position, resulting in linear relationship 
![[Pasted image 20250312122510.png]]

### Unilateral stance

Act as [[Kinetics#^095fa6|first class levers]], providing anticlockwise torque in form of **compression forces** during single limb support
![[Pasted image 20250312122331.png]]

- able to produce 2.5 - 4x body weight
#### Pelvic drop
Result of weak hip abductors, during single limb support, pelvis will drop towards side that is unsupported, hip abductors ==on contralateral side== cannot provide sufficient compression forces to realign pelvis 

Causes unstable gait pattern(Trendelenberg gait) → ↑ degeneration, fall risk etc
![[Pasted image 20250312135653.png|550]]

Use of walking stick: left side drop → right side abductors weak(affected) → walking stick on left side(unaffected)

## External Internal Rotators



Primary External rotators:
- gluteus medius posterior fibers
- piriformis

Internal rotators:
- gluteus medius ant fibers
- gluteus minimus ant fibers
- TFL
- adductors

### switching between ER & IR
when hip flexed to >60°, some ERs change to IRs because of attachments, causing change in line of pull during contraction. 

e.g. 
- piriformis
- Gluteus medius posterior fibers
- Gluteus maximus ant/sup fibers

[Example of piriformis as IR](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jNVR7uKJIzA) 


#### application
To stretch external rotators e.g. piriformis, put hip into flexion, switch to internal rotator → fibers can be lengthened in an externally rotated position


### adding to hip stability
Smaller muscles e.g. obturator internus can contribute to hip stability via compression forces between femoral head and acetabulum. 
During [[Hip movement#pelvic-on-femoral hip movement|pelvic-on-femoral external rotation]], muscles pull pelvis close to femur, generating compression forces into joint.
![[Pasted image 20250312135245.png]]
### adductor strain
During single limb support, adductors already active in stabilizing pelvis, then body turns → hip external rotation moment via [[Hip movement#pelvic-on-femoral hip movement|pelvic-on-femoral movement]], adductors have to act eccentrically, causing strain
