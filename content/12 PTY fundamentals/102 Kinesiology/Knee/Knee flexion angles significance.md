
 [[Knee ligaments#^6e01d6|ACL]] 2 bundles: anteromedial bundle (AMB) & posterolateral bundle (PLB). Depending on  position of knee, will affect which bundle fibers will be taut. 
 Extension → PLB  taut AMB lax
 Flexion → PLB lax, AMB taut 
 At approximately ==30° of the flexion neither of the bundles of the are taut leading to the most anterior translation== available at this range. It is most commonly injured in [[04 PTY/111 MSK 1/Knee/Ligamentous injuries|twisting movements]].  


[[Knee ligaments#^6e01d6|PCL]] similarly has 2 bundles: posteromedial (PMB) & anterolateral bundle (ALB). 
Extension: ALB larger + stronger are lax, PMB are taut 
80-90° Flexion: PMB are lax and  ALB taut. 
PCL is more adept for resisting posterior translation / sheering forces in the knee ==when it is flexed== despite there being the most posterior translation available at 75-90° flexion.

[[Knee ligaments#^6e01d6|MCL]] resist a valgus stress ==more effectively in closed pack position== (extension) due to the ==laxity of ligament in open packed position (flexed)==. 
- Secondary role in restraining anterior translation of the [tibia](https://www.physio-pedia.com/Tibia "Tibia") on the [femur](https://www.physio-pedia.com/Femur "Femur"). Therefore when someone has an [MCL injury](https://www.physio-pedia.com/Medial_Collateral_Ligament_Injury_of_the_Knee "Medial Collateral Ligament Injury of the Knee") the protection of the [anterior cruciate ligament](https://www.physio-pedia.com/Anterior_Cruciate_Ligament_\(ACL\) "Anterior Cruciate Ligament (ACL)") needs to be considered.

similarly to the MCL is most effective in full extension. another similarity of the [MCL](https://www.physio-pedia.com/Medial_Collateral_Ligament_of_the_Knee "Medial Collateral Ligament of the Knee") and the [LCL](https://www.physio-pedia.com/Lateral_Collateral_Ligament_of_the_Knee "Lateral Collateral Ligament of the Knee") is the ability of the [LCL](https://www.physio-pedia.com/Lateral_Collateral_Ligament_of_the_Knee "Lateral Collateral Ligament of the Knee") to also resist lateral rotation of the [tibia](https://www.physio-pedia.com/Tibia "Tibia") on the [femur](https://www.physio-pedia.com/Femur "Femur").
