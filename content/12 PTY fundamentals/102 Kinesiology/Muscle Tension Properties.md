## Principles affecting active tension in muscles
### 1. Physiological cross-sectional area(PSCA)
Area of muscle cross section perpendicular to every muscle fibre in segment
Different from Anatomical CSA → CSA at widest point of muscle
Different muscle types will have ACSA and PCSA


| Parallel                                      | Pennate                                                    |
| --------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| muscle fibres run in same direction as tendon | Muscle fibres run at angle from tendon                     |
| PCSA = ACSA                                   | PCSA > ACSA                                                |
| Fusiform e.g. bicep brachii, sartorius        | Unipennate e.g. EDL, ED,FPL, bipennate e.g. rectus femoris |

### 2. Pennation angle
Angle between muscle fibres and tendon
Higher Pennation angle means less force transmit through tendon = reduced force generation
But ==reduction in force not so significant== compared to generation by Pennate muscles

![[Pasted image 20250126205114.png]]

### 3. Active Tensions in muscles
Sarcomeres , myosin + actin filaments = basic unit of muscle fibre
More sarcomeres shortening = stronger contraction = ↑Tension

Relate to
![[Active Length Tension Curve]]

At ==optimal length==, most amount of sarcomeres contracting to produce maximal force
Note: not all muscles follow inverted U shape in example

When stretched, tension also produced and can increase until point of failure via viscoelastic principle

### 4. Force-velocity relationship
2 parts:
1. Concentric:
	Lesser weight (F) = Able to move to shortened position faster (V)
2. Eccentric
	More weight (F) = Fibres lengthen faster(V)

### 5. Number of [[motor units]] firing 
Recruitment principle
Depends on the area of stimulus, larger muscle stimulated → more muscle fibres activated
e.g. muscle belly of biceps brachii vs proximal end of biceps brachii
More force in muscle belly
### 6. Size of [[motor units]]
size principle
Cross sectional area + number of myofibrils
Smallest [[motor units]] activated first, ↑ intensity/demand = larger fibres activated
Type 1(least force) → Type 2a →Type 2x (most force)
### 7. Frequency of firing [[motor units]] 
Excitatory input/rate coding principle AP fire → 1 single twitch

If not fully relaxed:
Subsequent AP firing add to contraction activity → generate more tension
Until maximum tension generated

↑ rate of AP stimulations on muscles = Muscle fibres stay contracted until max tension generated and accumulate force produced aka fused tetanus

### Muscle/peripheral fatigue
1. [[Acidosis]]
2. Inorganic phosphate accumulation
	a. compete with ADP on [[Molecular Anatomy#Myosin|myosin]] sites
	b. bind with Ca$^{2+}$, less available for contraction
3. Ca$^{2+}$ leaks out cell,  SR storage deplete
4. Glycogen reserves depleted, less energy resources for metabolism
