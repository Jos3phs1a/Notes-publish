#### Class 1
- **Insulin** (rapid-acting, short-acting, intermediate, long-acting)
- **Sulphonylureas** (e.g., glibenclamide, glipizide)
- **Meglitinides** (e.g., repaglinide)

drugs that help pancreas to release more insulin or body to use glucose more effectively

**Significance**
- Most ==effective for **type 1 diabetes**== (insulin-dependent).
- Also used in **type 2 diabetes** when oral agents fail.
- Rapidly lowers blood glucose but carries a **risk of hypoglycemia** and **weight gain**

--- column-break ---

#### Class 2
- **Metformin** (Biguanide) - ==often used first== for [[Diabetes#Type 2|T2DM]]
- **Thiazolidinediones (TZDs)** – e.g., pioglitazone
- **α-Glucosidase inhibitors** – e.g., acarbose

drugs ==improve insulin sensitivity== in tissues or ==slow carbohydrate absorption== in the gut.

**Significance**
- **reduce insulin resistance**, improve lipid profiles, and may aid in **weight management**.
- Low risk of hypoglycemia.

--- column-break ---

#### Class 3
- **DPP-4 inhibitors** (e.g., sitagliptin, linagliptin)
- **GLP-1 receptor agonists** (e.g., liraglutide, exenatide)
- **SGLT2 inhibitors** (e.g., empagliflozin, dapagliflozin)

enhance ==glucose-dependent insulin secretion==, delay gastric emptying, or promote glucose loss through urine

**Significance**
- also used to prevent [[#Presence of complications|complications]] e.g. CVD, AKD
- weight loss and low risk of hypoglycemia.
- add-on therapy ==when metformin alone is insufficient.==

## Insulin therapy
short term intervention for: 
- [[diabetic emergencies]]
- uncontrolled hyperglycemia which may lead to [[glucotoxicity]] 
- symptomatic hyperglycemia
- catabolic features e.g. weight loss, [[muscular conditions#Atrophy|atrophy]], depletion of energy using fats and proteins
